Focus on Step 4
Strategy recommendation

The first three steps describe — the market, its analysis, the positioned project. Step 4 takes a stance: it answers “what now”, as an analyst would facing this file.

What this step is for

This step is for whoever has to decide — the entrepreneur weighing their launch, the investor assessing the file. It re-reads everything the pipeline has established (the market reading, its framework analysis, the project's positioning) and draws argued recommendations from it: what to lean on, at what price, through which channel, who to ally with — down to exit scenarios.

Like the other steps, each execution produces a dated, frozen report, listed on the market's page. The project evolves, the market moves: you re-run, and the successive recommendations stay comparable.

How to launch it

In Claude Code, enter “launch skill 4”, with the following inputs:

  • The positioned project — chosen among the projects that went through Step 3 on a studied market.
  • Optional: the marketing-mix choices already settled (product, price, promotion, place) — they will be judged on the evidence, not rewritten.
  • Optional — useful constraints (budget, timeline…), cited as such.

Prerequisite: a Step 3 run on the same market and the same project — Step 4 starts from an established positioning, never from a blank page. The full Step 1 → 2 → 3 chain is therefore in place by construction.

The deliverable

The report reads in this order.

1 · The market, as of the data's date

The backdrop in half a page, taken from Steps 1-2 with their dates: the market definition, its size (~ owned), the structure by sub-sectors and their shares, the dynamics — growth, structural shocks, capital movements — and the forces that govern it. The investor knows within minutes what the project fits into, on data whose freshness is displayed.

2 · The project's positioning

Taken from Step 3: the project in two lines, its values on each of the dimensions that structure the market (declarative, and marked as such), and its position among the players — its immediate neighbours, the space it occupies, the blind spot it targets. With the link to the interactive chart where the project appears among its competitors (✺ marker).

3 · The SWOT

The pipeline's first “project point of view” reading — which is precisely why it was excluded from the earlier steps: a SWOT with no reference product means nothing. Strengths and weaknesses look inward — the product promise, the stage, what remains declarative; opportunities and threats look outward — the market structure, the waves under way, competition, regulation.

Each entry is anchored in a fact — sourced by the runs or declared by the founder — never a generality.

4 · The marketing mix (4Ps)

One block per P — Product, Price, Promotion, Place (distribution). Each block follows the same path: the supporting data (cited from the runs: competitors' effective prices, sub-sector structure, support models…), the reasoning, then the conclusion in one of two forms:

  • The founder declared a choice → an argued verdict on that choice — agree or not, and why.
  • No declared choice → a recommendation, quantified as soon as the data allows — for example: the price range of competing offers, the reserve-price signal, then the recommended range and the suggested subscription format.

5 · Essential recommendations & partnerships

First the short prioritized list of what matters beyond the 4Ps — target, launch, communication, distribution — each item: recommendation + rationale. Then the section an M&A analyst would read first: the partnership or exit targets — named companies, and for each the strategic logic (why it, for this project), the plausible deal type (major partnership, acquisition, merger, equity stake) and the observable signals — comparable deals, stated strategies. It's the only section of the report fed by web research, sources cited.

Reading conventions

  • ~ estimated · — not found, as everywhere on the site.
  • Declarative values (the founder's) distinguished from sourced values (the runs).
  • A recommendation always shows its degree of uncertainty; a declared choice gets a frank verdict — when the data contradicts it, the report says so.
  • Report frozen at its date; the freshness of upstream data is shown at the top.

Essential additional information

The step's methodology, in brief:

  • Pure synthesis of the dated runs: the market-state sections launch no new research — the data's age is shown at the top, and beyond 3 months the report flags it and suggests re-running the upstream steps.
  • A single recourse to the web: the partnerships & exits section, because plausible acquirers spill beyond the studied market — each external fact is cited there with URL and date, distinctly from facts drawn from the runs.
  • Frank verdicts: choices declared by the founder are judged on the data; when the data contradicts them, the report says so.

The link to each report appears in the Step 4 section of the market's page (for example Mobile weight-loss assistance apps), like the runs of the other steps. The report is self-contained: it reads on its own, and points to the source runs for detail.
