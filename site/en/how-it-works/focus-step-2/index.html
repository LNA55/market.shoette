Focus on Step 2
Present the Market

The business reading of the market: what it's for, how to launch it, and how to read the report it produces.

What this step is for

This step presents the market in business language: it lays out its headline indicators (size, sub-sectors, dominant player, geography) then sheds light on it with the strategic analysis frameworks relevant to that particular market. Where Step 1 clears the ground and maps it, Step 2 structures and interprets — it prepares the ground for positioning your product (Step 3) and for the recommendations (Step 4).

How to launch it

In Claude Code, enter “launch skill 2”. The step starts from Step 1's work: it offers the markets already read and you choose which one to present with one click. It then relies on that market's latest Step 1 run and only complements with online research the information it lacks (sub-sector sizes, the dominant player's financials). If the Step 1 reading is more than three months old, it flags this and offers to refresh it first. Each launch produces a dated run.

The deliverable

The report is organized in three movements.

1 — The headline business KPIs

A fixed-structure table, the same from one market to the next so they stay comparable: market definition and main sub-sectors; global size and the size of each sub-sector (with its share, closed to 100%); the dominant established player — creation date, revenue, and profit stated clearly (loss-making / profitable / breaking even); and the market's geography.

2 — Remarks on the market and choice of methodology

Before analyzing, the agent explains which frameworks it retained and why — one remark per line, linking a market fact to the methodological choice it drives. This is what makes the analysis auditable: you know why a given tool was used and another set aside.

3 — The market through each framework

One section per retained framework: the question it answers, the analysis of the market through that lens (fed by the Step 1 figures), and a visualization filled with the market's actual data. The sources follow at the end. The possible frameworks, their questions and their KPIs are documented below.

Reading conventions

  • ~ before a value: estimate — frequent here, since many players are private companies.
  • —: information not found, and stated as such.
  • The sub-sector shares close to 100% thanks to a “rest of the market” line.
  • A framework in qualitative reading is flagged: it is retained, but for lack of sufficient quantified data it is not quantified.
  • Like every report on the site, a run is frozen at its date.

The frameworks, one by one

TAM / SAM / SOM

Standard vocabulary of venture capital and business planning.

“Is the pie worth the effort? Is the market expanding or declining?”

Three nested circles to size a market. The TAM (Total Addressable Market) is the total theoretical demand if everyone bought. The SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market) narrows it to the customers your type of offer can serve technically and geographically. The SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) is what you can realistically capture in 3-5 years, given the competition and your means — the only one of the three figures that commits you.

Combined with the CAGR and the maturity stage, it tells you whether the game is worth the candle. Classic trap: inflated TAMs computed “top-down” (x% of a giant market) — prefer the “bottom-up” calculation: number of reachable customers × realistic annual basket.

Porter — 5 forces

Michael Porter, Harvard Business School, 1979.

“Is this market structurally profitable? Who holds the power?”

A sector's profitability doesn't depend only on the players' talent: it's structural. Five forces determine it — the intensity of rivalry between competitors, the threat of new entrants, the threat of substitute products, the bargaining power of suppliers and that of buyers.

The stronger the forces, the more margins are under pressure. The useful reading: identify where the power lies in the chain, hence who captures the value — and whether a defensible position is possible there (barriers to entry, switching costs, brand).

↑ Back to the table

Value chain

Michael Porter, “Competitive Advantage”, 1985.

“Where is margin captured along the chain?”

Porter's canonical model represents the firm as a sequence of five primary activities — inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing & sales, service — supported by four support functions that run across the whole chain: firm infrastructure, human resource management, technology development, procurement. The margin is what remains when the value created exceeds the cost of all these activities.

Applied to an entire market, the same logic reads link by link (production, distribution, retail…): where is margin captured? The finding is often counter-intuitive — the most visible link isn't necessarily the one making money. The view is completed on the customer side: the CAC (what acquiring a customer costs) against the LTV (what they bring over their lifetime), with an LTV/CAC ratio above 3 as the healthy threshold.

↑ Back to the table

BCG Matrix

Bruce Henderson, Boston Consulting Group, 1970.

“Where are the players' strong/weak positions?”

A 2×2 matrix crossing market growth (attractiveness) with relative market share — your share divided by the leader's (a measure of strength). Four cells: stars (invest), cash cows (milk to fund the rest), question marks (decide quickly) and dogs (divest).

Applied to a market's players rather than to a portfolio, it reveals who holds a strong position on which segment — and which segments a challenger can attack without facing the leader head-on.

Perceptual map

Perceptual mapping — classic marketing, 1960s-70s.

“How are offers perceived? Where are the empty spaces?”

Positioning offers on attribute axes as customers perceive them — price, quality, style, service — from surveys, customer reviews or NPS. The map shows the saturated territories and, above all, the empty spaces.

An empty space is either a positioning opportunity or the absence of demand — only research settles it. It's exactly the logic of Step 1's interactive positioning chart, which encodes up to five dimensions simultaneously.

Vitality (deal-flow view)

An investor-style analysis grid — not an academic framework.

“Does smart money believe in it? How fast is it being disrupted?”

Reading a market's vitality through money and movement: amounts raised over 12-24 months, number and size of acquisitions, pace of company creation, R&D intensity (patents, % of revenue invested).

“Smart money” anticipates: a sector that raises a lot will transform fast; a sector with no deals at all is asleep — or locked down by its incumbents. M&A also tells you who's consolidating: when the audience leader buys the AI innovator of the moment, that's a market signal in its own right.

↑ Back to the table

PESTEL

Derived from Francis Aguilar's PEST analysis (1967), extended with the environmental and legal dimensions.

“Which external forces can kill or boost the market?”

Sweep six families of macro forces — Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal — that bear on all players, regardless of their merits.

It's the framework of exogenous shocks. Example from this site's first run: the arrival of GLP-1 drugs — a shock that is at once technological, regulatory and sociocultural — sent the historic leader of weight-loss apps into bankruptcy in under two years.

↑ Back to the table

GE / McKinsey 9-box

McKinsey for General Electric, 1970s — in response to the limits of the BCG matrix.

“Which segments deserve investment?”

A 3×3 matrix crossing market attractiveness (composite score: size × growth × margin) with competitive strength (market share × brand × cost structure). Nine cells, three decision zones: invest/grow, selectivity, harvest/divest.

More nuanced than the BCG (composite scores rather than two raw KPIs), but more subjective too: the weighting of the criteria is a choice — to be made explicit so the analysis stays auditable.

↑ Back to the table

SWOT

Attributed to the work of Albert Humphrey, Stanford, 1960s.

“Conclusion: should you go in, and where?”

Four quadrants crossing the internal — strengths and weaknesses — with the external — opportunities and threats. Its value isn't to bring new data: it's the synthesis tool that organizes everything the previous frameworks established.

Used well, it comes last and each box cites its sources (the KPIs of the upstream analyses). Used badly, it comes first and fills up with hunches — that's the trap to avoid.

Place in the pipeline: the SWOT doesn't enter the analysis produced by Step 2 — it requires a point of view (“my product”) and concludes Steps 3 and 4. It's documented here with the other frameworks.

Include or exclude: what the market decides

Step 2 doesn't apply every framework to every market. Three inputs guide the selection: the nature of the market as Step 1 established it (maturity, B2C or B2B, value chain, regulation, segments, deal flow), the availability of the data each framework's KPIs require, and the intent of the analysis (in V1: assess the market opportunity).

Three possible levels: included · downgraded to qualitative (retained, but without figures — and it says so) · excluded. The report shows only the retained frameworks.

FrameworkIncluded when the market shows…Excluded or downgraded when…
TAM / SAM / SOM Size estimates exist, even as a range, and the goal is to assess the opportunity. A market so nascent that any figure would be fictional → excluded.
Porter — 5 forces By default — it's the structural foundation of any market analysis. Emerging market with no formed structure → partial reading of the forces already legible.
Value chain A chain with several links: production, distribution, retail… Short chain (apps, SaaS) → switches to the “unit economics” variant: CAC/LTV, platform tolls.
BCG Matrix Segments that are distinct and measurable — the reading is done at the segment level. Redundant with the share × growth chart (player level) of the Step 1 run → excluded.
Perceptual map A B2C market with a strong brand component, with perception data (customer reviews, NPS). Without survey data → qualitative reading pointing to the S1 run's interactive chart. B2B or commodity with no perceived differentiation → excluded.
Vitality Fundraising or M&A spotted by Step 1 — or an absence that is itself meaningful. Sector with no investment ecosystem → reduced to a single observation line.
PESTEL A regulated market, sensitive to the macro, or an external shock already spotted by Step 1. Stable market with no notable external exposure → lightened version.
GE / McKinsey 9-box At least 3 identifiable segments, and enough data for composite scores. Identifiable segments but thin data → qualitative judgment. No segments → excluded.
SWOT Out of Step 2's scope by decision: it requires a point of view (“my product”) and concludes Steps 3 and 4.
