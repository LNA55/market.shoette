01

The project

What is wrong with me — w.shoette.com — is an AI conversational health journal ("The AI health journal you talk to"): you speak to it — voice, photos, medical documents — it connects to everyday signals (sleep, stress, cycle, weather, calendar) and surfaces weighted correlations to answer "why I'm not feeling well". The project is in beta on a waitlist, explicitly declares itself "not a medical device", and hosts its data in the European Union (GDPR, HDS).

The use case positioned here declared : the investigation into weight-loss stalling — people with obesity (BMI > 30) stuck at a high weight despite numerous attempts; primary test target: GLP-1 users (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro) who observe neither significant weight loss nor appetite suppression.

Project values in this note are declarative — drawn from the project website and Elena's framing — unlike competitor values, which are sourced publicly by run S1.

03

Chart analysis

On the default configuration (market share on the vertical axis), What is wrong with me sits at the very bottom of the chart, at 0% — a mechanical result for a waitlist project; that is not where the reading lies. On the structural dimensions, the project occupies a position held by no other player in the panel. On medical integration (~3/10), it stands alone in the market's empty corridor: the panel splits into two blocks — the mainstream trackers and coaches between 0 and 2 (MyFitnessPal, Simple, Cal AI, Yazio, Lose It!, MacroFactor, Lifesum, Cronometer) and the medicalised duo at 9 (WeightWatchers, Noom); between 3 and 8, no one. The only players who might come close — GLP-1 companions such as Shotsy or MeAgain, around 5 — are precisely those for which run S1 found no hard data: the project's nearest neighbours are invisible on the chart. On AI personalisation (~9/10, declarative), the project joins the top trio alongside Simple (9), Cal AI (8) and Noom (8) — with a difference in kind: for them, the AI executes a programme (log, coach, adjust targets); here, it conducts an investigation (correlating hundreds of signals to explain a stall). On support model, it falls in with the AI coaches (Simple, MacroFactor, MyFitnessPal, Lifesum); on functional breadth, it shares the single-function slot only with Cal AI — two single-use, high-AI products with opposite purposes: photographing calories vs understanding why nothing is working. Price is the only dimension without a value (not announced). Finally, the declared target — BMI > 30, persistent stalling, and first and foremost GLP-1 users with no marked response — places the project in the market's blind spot as read by runs S1/S2: medicalised platforms (61% of the market) sell access to treatment, the nascent GLP-1 companions (~1% of the market) organise its follow-up, but no one addresses those for whom the treatment fails to deliver on its promise.

  • The 3–8 medical integration corridor is empty: between mainstream trackers (0–2) and prescription platforms (9), only What is wrong with me stands there — alongside GLP-1 companions, off the chart for lack of data.
  • The project's AI competes in the market's top trio (~9/10, declarative, with Simple, Cal AI and Noom) — but for a function no competitor offers: investigating causes rather than executing a programme.
  • Its only structural single-function × high-AI neighbour is Cal AI — with exactly the opposite purpose: logging calories vs explaining a stall.
  • 0% share and price not announced: the position signals potential, not traction — all project values are declarative, to be verified at launch.
