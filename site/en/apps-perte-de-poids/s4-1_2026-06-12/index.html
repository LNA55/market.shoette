01

The market, as of June 11, 2026

The market for consumer apps whose core proposition is weight-loss assistance is worth ~$2.2 billion (2025), growing at ~11-13%/year — but this is a market in structural transition, not quiet expansion: value is rising (+13% in-app revenue in 2025) while volume stagnates (+0.8% downloads). Winning is no longer about raw acquisition; it is about value captured per user.

  • Structure: medicalized platforms (app + teleconsultation + GLP-1) capture ~61% of value; calorie tracking ~20% (commoditized by AI photo-logging); behavioral & AI coaching ~9%; dedicated GLP-1 companions ~1% — a nascent category with no leader.
  • The structural shock: GLP-1s — used for weight loss by 12.4% of American adults, more than doubled in 18 months — are a pharmacological substitute for "behavioral dieting" itself. They drove the historic market leader (WeightWatchers, 31% share, est. 1963) into rapid bankruptcy in May 2025, from which it emerged debt-free and repositioned as a medical player.
  • Competitive pressure: the two leaders (WeightWatchers ~31%, Noom ~29%) concentrate ~60% of revenue; structurally high churn, near-zero switching costs; Apple and Google collect a 15-30% toll on every subscription.
  • Capital is active: in 24 months — WW bankruptcy and restructuring, two MyFitnessPal acquisitions (Intent, then Cal AI in December 2025), Simple's $35 million Series B, Noom's 503A pharmacy acquisition (April 2026), a wave of new GLP-1 companion entrants (Shotsy, MeAgain, Pep). Direction of capital flows: low-friction AI and medical integration.
  • The lesson from recent trajectories: a genuinely differentiated entrant reaches ~1-2% of the market in 3-5 years (Cal AI: ~$30 million in revenue in 18 months in the photo-logging niche; MacroFactor: ~$24 million in macro-tracking).

All values ~estimated — synthesis of runs S1-2 and S2-1 from June 11, 2026.

02

Project positioning

What is wrong with me (w.shoette.com) is an AI-powered conversational health journal: you speak to it — voice, photos, medical documents — and it correlates hundreds of signals to explain a blockage. Positioned here on the use case declared by Elena: investigating a weight-loss plateau — BMI > 30, repeated failures, and first and foremost GLP-1 users without a meaningful response. Beta on waiting list, "not a medical device," data hosted in the EU.

  • It occupies the market's empty corridor alone: medical integration ~3/10, in the 3-8 corridor that no one else fills — its closest conceptual neighbors, the GLP-1 companions, are absent from the chart for lack of public data.
  • Its AI ranks in the top three (~9/10, declarative — alongside Simple, Cal AI, Noom), but for a function unseen in this market: investigating root causes rather than executing a program.
  • Deliberately single-function (alone with Cal AI): no calorie tracking, no plans, no community — the app does one thing: understand why progress is blocked.
  • 0% market share, price unannounced: the position signals potential, not traction — all values are declarative.

SWOT

Internal (strengths, weaknesses) × external (opportunities, threats) — each entry anchored to the run that supports it.

Internal — the project

S · Strengths

  • Alone in the market's middle corridor (medical integration ~3, corridor 3-8 empty) S3
  • Multimodal conversational AI at the level of the top three (~9/10) S3 — declarative
  • Function unseen in this market: explaining the blockage rather than prescribing a program S3
  • EU privacy displayed (GDPR, HDS, AES-256) on highly sensitive data declarative

W · Weaknesses

  • Beta on waiting list: product promise entirely declarative, nothing verified in production S3
  • 0% share, zero brand recognition against players with tens of millions of users S1
  • No tracker, no program: risk of unmet expectations in this market (single-function) S3
  • Price undefined; "not a medical device" in a market that is 61% medicalized S3 + S2
External — the market

O · Opportunities

  • The GLP-1 wave (12.4% of US adults) creates a massive treated population — and thus treatment disappointees, the market's blind spot S2 + S3
  • GLP-1 companion category nascent (~1%), with no leader S2
  • Dense deal flow — the market acquires innovation rather than replicating it (Cal AI precedent) S2
  • Weakened leaders: WW −20%, MyFitnessPal −16% growth S1

T · Threats

  • Intense rivalry: top 2 = ~60% of revenue, high churn, near-zero switching costs S2
  • WW Clinic / Noom Med can add a "why isn't it working" module S1
  • Store toll: 15-30% of every subscription S2
  • If treatments improve (higher doses, oral forms), the non-responder segment may shrink S2

Reading. The project's singular strength — being alone on the question "why is it blocked" in a market that only knows how to prescribe programs — meets a real opportunity: the GLP-1 wave mechanically produces treatment disappointees, and the category that could serve them has no leader. But the two critical weaknesses mirror each other: everything is declarative (beta), and the project has neither the brand recognition nor the resources of a market where the top 2 capture 60% of value. The resulting strategy is almost self-evident: produce evidence fast (every successful investigation is an asset), in a narrow, painful niche that the giants do not address — before WW Clinic or Noom Med move in, and before treatments improve.

Marketing mix — recommendations

No mix choices declared by Elena → all 4 Ps receive quantified recommendations where the runs allow.

Product

Supporting data: single-function positioning × AI ~9 (S3); pure calorie tracking is commoditized, declining attractiveness (S2 — GE/McKinsey); the "GLP-1 companion" box is the "invest" box for an agile entrant (S2); defensible position requires: medical integration or organic AI differentiation (S2 — Porter).

Recommendation: remain an investigation product, and build the GLP-1 bridge — without crossing the medical line. Do not add calorie tracking or programs: that is the market's commoditized box, and dilution would destroy the project's only defensible position (investigation). On the other hand, for the launch target, the treatment journal must become a first-class signal: injection dates, dose, felt effects, correlated with everything else — that is precisely the "understand why it's not responding" promise. Complete this with an "investigation dossier" export for medical consultations: it reinforces the status of a complement (not a substitute), defuses the "not a medical device" constraint, and turns every physician into a potential recommender of the app.

Price

Supporting data (S1, effective monthly prices, ~estimated): market range $2.50-23/month; mean ~$7.80/month, median ~$5.50/month; two clear blocks — consumer trackers and coaches $2.50-6.70/month (Cal AI $2.50, Lose It! $3.30, Yazio $4, Simple $4.50, Cronometer $5, MacroFactor/Lifesum $6, MyFitnessPal $6.70), medicalized programs $17.40-23/month (Noom, WeightWatchers). S2: per-user value growing (+13%), profitability demonstrated at both ends of the price spectrum.

Recommendation: ~$9-14/month on annual subscription — above trackers, below programs. The market's reservation price shows that the "beyond simple tracking" promise commands a premium: the medicalized block holds at $17-23/month. Without clinicians, the project cannot claim that block — but its personalized investigation promise is worth a clear premium over a $5 logger. Concretely: ~$12/month billed annually (~$144/year) as the reference price at public launch, a pricier no-commitment monthly (~$19/month) to steer users toward annual, and the free beta for the GLP-1 test cohort — at this stage, the value to capture is proof, not revenue. Pricing range to revisit once the first successful investigations land: a correlation that unlocks months of wandering could justify the opposite — an openly premium price.

Promotion

Supporting data: high acquisition cost in a volume-saturated market (+0.8% downloads) — the proven path is organic (Cal AI virality: 15 million downloads in under two years, built by two high-school students) (S2 — Porter, TAM/SAM/SOM); medicalization destigmatizes demand, discourse shifts from "diet" to "treatment" (S2 — PESTEL); S3 target: precise, identifiable, high-pain.

Recommendation: zero paid acquisition at launch — a proof that tells its own story. The market punishes paid (high CAC, high churn) and rewards differentiated organic. Three levers aligned with the product: (1) the investigations themselves as content — every correlation discovered (anonymized, consented) is a story the target audience instantly recognizes, the project's organic engine; (2) the founder-patient voice — Elena belongs to the audience she is targeting (information provided by Elena): in a market that is destigmatizing, "I am looking for my own answer" authenticity carries more weight than any ad — to use if and only if she feels comfortable doing so; (3) EU privacy as a trust differentiator: on data this intimate, "never sold, never used to train someone else's AI" is a competitive edge against the US panel giants.

Place (distribution)

Supporting data: Apple and Google collect 15-30% of every subscription — the first margin link surrendered by the entire ecosystem (S2 — value chain); the project's beta is already web-first (waiting list at w.shoette.com — S3); B2B2C signals in section 5 (employer GLP-1 programs).

Recommendation: web-first for the beta, app stores at public launch, B2B2C as a second stage. Keep the beta on the web: zero toll, fast iteration, direct relationship with the test cohort. At public launch, the stores become unavoidable (daily mobile use is the panel norm) — but maintain web subscription as the reference channel to protect margin wherever possible. Do not scatter toward the employer/insurer channel during the beta — but prepare it: that is where the "detect non-responders early" promise has direct economic value (cf. section 5).

05

Key recommendations & partnerships

The recommendations that matter beyond the mix

  • 1 · Hold the niche, resist expansion. The project is by nature a general health app; in this market, its strength lies in attacking via the most painful and least-served segment — GLP-1 non-responders (the blind spot established by S3). Expanding ("health app for everyone") before having evidence would dilute the only defensible position. Generalization will come from proof, not the other way around.
  • 2 · Proof before growth. Weakness #1 (everything is declarative) is corrected neither by marketing nor by price: only the beta corrects it. Recommended tracking metric: time to first useful correlation per user — that is the product promise made measurable, and the number investors and partners will ask for.
  • 3 · Make investigation data an asset. Architect from the beta onward the consented, anonymized, aggregated production of insights on GLP-1 non-response — that is precisely the data pharma, telehealth, and payers are looking for (cf. targets below), and the foundation of a defensible position that the giants cannot replicate by copying the interface.
  • 4 · Prepare for replication. WW Clinic and Noom Med have the audience and the incentive to add a "why isn't it working" module. The project's window is short: it is defended by speed (published evidence), claimed specialization, and — if traction comes — by aligning with one of the partners below before replication arrives.

Partnership & exit targets — M&A view

This section draws on a web search conducted June 12, 2026 (the only section of the report permitted to do so) — every external fact is sourced. Founding fact: the non-responder segment is clinically documented — up to ~23-25% of patients on semaglutide lose less than 5% of their weight, and a higher dose does not reduce that proportion [Scientific American, 2025-10-27; Taylor & Francis review 2025]. A quarter of treated patients, with no organized market response: that is the project's hunting ground — and the central argument in every line below.

TargetPlausible operationStrategic logic & observable signals
Novo Nordisk major partnership The most directly aligned partner: its Partner Platform (NNPP) explicitly seeks coaching, scanning, and body composition apps to improve GLP-1 outcomes [pharmaceutical-technology.com, 2025-06-18]; network rebuilt at pace — WeightWatchers/NovoCare (Jul. 2025), Talkspace/WeGoTogether, Hims & Hers deal (Mar. 2026) [pharmaceuticalcommerce.com; statnews.com, 2026-03-09]. An app that explains non-response addresses THEIR problem: 25% disappointed patients equals 25% medication churn.
Eli Lilly partnership (integration) Same logic on Zepbound/Mounjaro, but a different model: LillyDirect integrates third-party partners rather than acquiring [prnewswire.com, 2024-01-04]; just integrated Omada into its Employer Connect (May 2026). The entry point: appear in that ecosystem as a "treatment response" module.
Oura acquisition / data partnership The hottest signal of the group — and a budding threat: Oura acquired Veri, a metabolic health app (Sept. 2024) and just launched "GLP-1 Insights" in Ring 5 (May 2026) — dose, side effects, sleep correlation [businesswire.com, 2024-09-11; hitconsultant.net, 2026-05-29]. The ring captures the signals; W makes them speak. Maximum product complementarity — but if they build it alone, the window closes. Approach early (API partnership first).
Whoop acquisition / data partnership Parallel trajectory to Oura: acquisition of Anyot (non-invasive glucose, 2025), Advanced Labs with Quest (Sept. 2025), IPO contemplated — an acquirer seeking differentiated software layers before listing [insider.fitt.co; whoop.com, 2025-09].
Ro clinical partnership Ro has made its patient data a strategic asset: research collaboration with Amgen, 8 abstracts at ObesityWeek 2025, peer-reviewed publication [ro.co/press, 2025-10]. The natural bridge: W detects and documents non-responders → Ro adjusts the protocol. Co-studies are possible — and the "investigation dossier" export (cf. Product) is the vehicle.
WeightWatchers / Noom acquisition (product brick) The panel consolidators: WW is rebuilding as an integrated medical player (Sequence $132 million in 2023, NovoCare distributor 2025) [mobihealthnews.com]; Noom is expanding beyond weight — 503A pharmacy (Apr. 2026), SmartRx employer program, Highmark partnership [globenewswire.com, 2026-04-01; noom.com]. The precedent that counts: MyFitnessPal acquired Cal AI rather than replicating it (S2). A "non-response investigation" brick fits exactly into their medicalized offerings.
Omada Health / payers B2B2C distribution The channel where non-response has a quantifiable cost: Omada launches GLP-1 Flex Care for employers and integrates Lilly Employer Connect (May 2026) [investors.omadahealth.com, 2026-05]; Noom already sells employers the GLP-1 cost savings. W's argument for this channel: detecting a non-responder early means stopping payment for ~$1,000/month of ineffective treatment — the app pays for itself.

Outside the panel: comparable investigation apps (Bearable, Visible, Guava Health) remain independent — no acquisition recorded to date [crunchbase.com]: the "health investigation journal" category has not yet been consolidated. Being the first to be consolidated — or the consolidator — is a plausible outcome in 3-5 years.
