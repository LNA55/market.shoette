01

The project

What is wrong with me — w.shoette.com — is a conversational AI health journal (The AI health journal you talk to): you talk to it — voice, photos, medical documents — it aggregates daily signals (sleep, stress, cycle, weather, calendar) and its engine searches for correlations to answer "why am I feeling off." The app is free (€0/year), available on the Apple Store and Google Play, and works by invitation only. It declares itself "not a medical device" and hosts its data within the European Union (GDPR, HDS).

The business model declared by Elena: the app is the tool; the paid value is membership in a research programme — a "shared anonymised data pool around one issue" at €200/month, paid to a research company that organises calls and, above all, the data processing. Within a programme, the engine compares correlations across participants: as soon as a lead emerges, it proposes the same test to everyone (e.g., a lead "lactose after 5 pm → non-restorative sleep" triggers an instruction "take lactose the next day at 6 pm" — each participant remains free to accept or decline), and the validated finding is fed back into the app, which maintains a continuously re-evaluated profile score.

The use case positioned here declared by Elena: Programme 1 — the study of weight-loss stalling in patients on Mounjaro 15 mg (maximum dose) with no effect on hunger or cravings, alongside addictive sugar behaviour. That is, very precisely, full-dose GLP-1 non-responders: the market's blind spot identified by runs S1/S2.

Project values in this note are self-declared — drawn from the project website and Elena's brief — unlike competitor values, which were sourced publicly by run S1. This run S3-2 updates run S3-1 (12 June 2026) with the product information provided since then.

Medical credibility: how many participants for Programme 1?

A one-off analysis requested by Elena for this run — outside the standard Step 3 framework (not integrated into the skill or the contract).

There is no magic number. The credibility of a health finding depends first not on the number of participants, but on the ambition of the claim one wants to draw from it, weighed against three obstacles: inter-individual variability (in metabolism it is enormous — the ZOE PREDICT 1 study shows that genetics explains less than 30% of the variation in glycaemic responses to the same meal), multiplicity of tests (W's engine scans hundreds of correlations: without statistical discipline, "significant" associations are found by pure chance) and reproducibility (an effect is credible only if it repeats). Three tiers can therefore be outlined.

Tier 1 — Generate a lead
~12 to 30 participants
Well-characterised, per hypothesis. Enough to surface an exploratory signal or individual proof (n-of-1); the "12 per group" floor is the empirical rule of thumb for a pilot study (Julious, 2005). This is not medical proof — just a lead to confirm.
Tier 2 — Convince (publication)
~50 to 200 completers
Per hypothesis, in a repeated within-subject design (each participant is their own control), pre-registered and corrected for multiplicity; over a period of weeks to months. Demonstrates a real effect in this specific cohort — "publishable" level, not yet generalisable.
Tier 3 — Change practice
several hundred to ~1,000+
Pre-registered randomised controlled trial (RCT), 6 to 18 months, ideally replicated by an independent team. This is the scale at which the medical world considers an effect established and generalisable (cf. the GLP-1 trials below).

Good news for W: its protocol — proposing the same test to the entire cohort triggered by a lead (the "lactose at 6 pm"), then measuring the response — is exactly a crossover "challenge-rechallenge" trial, that is, an aggregated n-of-1. This design is recognised by medicine (AHRQ reference guide) and far more powerful per participant than a classical trial, because each person is their own control: it neutralises inter-individual variability, which is precisely the main obstacle here. W's credibility lever is therefore not the number but the rigour: pre-declared hypothesis, randomised test order, wash-out period, blinding where possible, repetition of cycles, and correction for multiple tests. For reference, here is what the medical world has required for comparable claims:

  • SURMOUNT-1 — tirzepatide (Mounjaro), the most directly comparable trial: 2,539 participants, 72 weeks, to establish Mounjaro's efficacy in obesity (Jastreboff et al., NEJM 2022). nejm.org ↗
  • STEP 1 — semaglutide (Wegovy): 1,961 participants, 68 weeks (Wilding et al., NEJM 2021). Same bar: ~2,000 people over ~16 months to establish a treatment's efficacy. nejm.org ↗
  • ZOE PREDICT 1 — the closest analogue to W (individual responses to food): ~1,000 participants (including twins), to map variability in postprandial responses (Berry et al., Nature Medicine 2020). The order of magnitude of a credible "personalised nutrition" dataset. nature.com ↗
  • DIETFITS — the warning on personalisation: 609 participants, 12 months, testing whether genotype or insulin predicts the right diet… and concluding with no significant interaction (Gardner et al., JAMA 2018). A compelling personalisation lead can evaporate at scale: hence the need to replicate. jamanetwork.com ↗
  • The n-of-1 framework: Design and Implementation of N-of-1 Trials: A User's Guide (Kravitz & Duan, AHRQ 2014) — the methodological reference that makes W's design defensible… if it follows the rules. ahrq.gov ↗
Recommendation for Programme 1 — aim, per hypothesis tested, for 50 to 100 well-phenotyped completers (Mounjaro 15 mg non-responders), each completing several test cycles: in a within-subject design, this is the realistic threshold for producing a persuasive and publishable signal on this cohort — without yet claiming a generalisable proof, which would require an RCT of several hundred participants over 6–18 months. The number-one pitfall is not size, it is multiplicity: with 40 participants and hundreds of correlations scanned, spurious "discoveries" accumulate rapidly. Credibility rests on three words: pre-register the hypothesis, correct for multiple tests, replicate before publishing.
